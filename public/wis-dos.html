    What is the Weather Intensity Score?

    The Weather Intensity Score (WIS) is an internal real-time weather monitoring algorithm developed by Ryan Hall. Think of it as a "weather intensity thermometer" that measures how severe and impactful weather is across the United States RIGHT NOW, at this very minute. Unlike the Daily Outlook Score which looks ahead, WIS tells the team what's happening in real-time.

    IMPORTANT NOTE: The Weather Intensity Score is primarily an internal tool used by the Ryan Hall, Y'all team. While the public may find it interesting to follow along, any personal decisions based on WIS should be made at your own discretion. Always consult official local weather warnings and radar for your area.

    How to Read the Score

    // WIS RANGE: 0-150+ // HIGHER VALUES INDICATE INCREASED SEVERITY

    0-30: Mostly Quiet

    Weather across the country is fairly calm with minimal RHY team activity.

    30-50: Some Activity

    Weather is happening somewhere and the team is monitoring closely.

    50-70: Notable Weather

    RHY team is paying close attention and standby mode is likely.

    70-100: Significant Events

    A developing situation; the team is often going live with coverage.

    100-150: Multiple Areas Impacted

    Full team coverage mode with several regions being affected.

    150-250: Major Outbreak

    An ongoing severe outbreak requiring all hands on deck.

    250-350: Extreme Event

    A high-end event in progress demanding continuous live coverage.

    350+: Potentially Historic

    Potentially historic events; scores above 500 have occurred during major outbreaks.

    Real-World Examples

    Some Activity (30-50)
    • A few severe thunderstorm warnings scattered across states
    • Perhaps 2-3 tornado warnings in rural areas
    • Minor winter weather advisories
    • Example: 3 tornado warnings in rural Oklahoma might score 40
    Significant Events (70-100)
    • Multiple tornado warnings across several states
    • Severe weather ramping up regionally
    • Major winter storm beginning to impact cities
    • Population centers starting to be affected
    Major Outbreak (150-250)
    • Numerous tornado warnings across a large region
    • Confirmed tornadoes on the ground in multiple states
    • Major cities under threat
    • Significant damage occurring
    Historic Event (350+)
    • March 14, 2025 outbreak (score exceeded 500)
    • Multiple, potentially violent tornadoes impacting populated areas
    • Potentially generational weather disaster
    • Regional to potentially national emergency level event

    What Makes the Score Go Up?

    • >
      Severe Weather Warnings: Tornado warnings have the biggest impact, especially in populated areas
    • >
      Population Impact: A tornado warning in a major city increases the score more than one in a rural area
    • >
      Live Verification: Storm chasers streaming, traffic cameras showing severe conditions
    • >
      Public Interest: Millions watching weather coverage indicates severity

    Critical Understanding

    WIS is a NATIONAL view of weather intensity. A single tornado warning in a rural area might only generate a score of 10-20, even though that tornado could be life-threatening for those in its path. Never use WIS for local weather decisions - always check your local warnings and radar.

    Key Points to Remember

    • •WIS measures weather happening RIGHT NOW, updated every 60 seconds
    • •Scores above 50-70 often trigger RHY team standby or live coverage
    • •High scores mean active weather somewhere in the US, not necessarily in your area
    • •Always use official warnings and local radar for safety decisions

    What is the Daily Outlook Score?

    The Daily Outlook Score (DOS) is an internal weather forecasting algorithm developed by Ryan Hall. Think of it as a "weather importance thermometer" - it measures how significant weather events will be over the next seven days across the United States. Unlike current weather alerts, DOS looks ahead to help you plan for what's coming.

    Important Note: The Daily Outlook Score is primarily an internal tool used by the Ryan Hall, Y'all team to determine plans around videos, live streams, forecasts, and potential relief missions. While the public may find it interesting to follow along, any personal decisions based on DOS should be made at your own discretion.

    How to Read the Score

    // DOS RANGE: 0-200+ // FORECAST IMPACT ASSESSMENT

    0-30: Mostly Quiet

    Weather day ahead; anything above 20 can still feature a surprise localized event.

    30-60: Some Activity

    Weather is possible; even intense storms in rural or small areas can land in this range.

    60-90: Worth Watching

    RHY team usually standing by for a live stream.

    90-120: Significant Weather

    Full-blown live coverage is typically underway.

    120-150: Major Event Expected

    High-confidence large-scale event is on the way.

    150-200: Extreme Weather

    Extreme impacts are expected soon; preparations are ramping up.

    200+: Potentially Historic

    Potentially historic event in the forecast window.

    What Makes the Score Go Up?

    Tropical Weather (Hurricanes)

    • • Storm intensity - major hurricanes score higher
    • • Distance to US - closer storms have bigger impact
    • • Population in path - urban areas multiply scores
    • • Formation probability for developing systems

    Severe Weather (Tornadoes, Hail, Wind)

    • • Risk categories - Enhanced Risk and above trigger high scores
    • • Significant severe "hatched" areas multiply impact
    • • Population affected - urban areas increase scores
    • • Multiple hazards in same area compound

    Winter Weather

    • • Impact severity - extreme impacts score highest
    • • Location matters - Southern/Eastern US scores higher
    • • Forecast confidence affects future day scores
    • • Area coverage - widespread events score exponentially

    Real-World Examples

    Quiet Day (DOS: 0-30)

    • Isolated thunderstorms in rural areas
    • Minor winter weather in well-prepared mountain regions
    • Tropical wave more than 2,000 miles from the US coast
    • General thunderstorms affecting fewer than one million people

    Some Activity (DOS: 30-60)

    • Scattered severe thunderstorm warnings in sparsely populated areas
    • Localized flash flooding potential from slow-moving storms
    • Strong coastal storm clips a small section of shoreline
    • Tropical storm forming far offshore with limited immediate impacts

    Worth Watching (DOS: 60-90)

    • Enhanced Risk severe weather approaching a major metro area
    • Category 1 hurricane 1,000-1,500 miles from the US coast
    • Major winter storm forecast to impact multiple northern states
    • Tropical disturbance with high formation odds near the Caribbean

    Major Event Coming (DOS: 120-150)

    • High Risk severe weather day – rare and extremely dangerous
    • Major hurricane (Category 3+) with likely US landfall within three days
    • Extreme winter impacts forecast for major Eastern cities
    • Category 4-5 hurricane approaching a populated coastline

    Historic Potential (DOS: 200+)

    • Category 5 hurricane bearing down on a major city like Miami or Houston
    • Historic tornado outbreak rivaling 2011 or 1974 events
    • Catastrophic ice storm targeting unprepared Southern cities
    • "Perfect storm" scenario with compounding extreme threats

    Key Takeaways

    • •DOS is a "weather importance thermometer" for the entire United States
    • •Scores above 60 typically mean RHY team preparing for active coverage
    • •Multiple factors drive scores: intensity, population impact, distance, confidence
    • •Same weather scores differently by location (Southern winter storms score higher)
    • •Updates every 5 minutes with new forecast data
